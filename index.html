Filing a Voluntary Petition

A plain-language guide to 11 U.S.C. Section 301 -- the starting point for every voluntary bankruptcy case. What you need, what it costs, and what happens the moment you file.

What Is a Voluntary Petition?

A voluntary petition is the document that starts a bankruptcy case. It is filed by the debtor -- the person or business seeking bankruptcy relief -- not by a creditor. Section 301 of the Bankruptcy Code authorizes any entity that may be a debtor under the applicable chapter to file a voluntary case.

The moment the petition is filed with the bankruptcy court, the case begins. A case number is assigned, the automatic stay takes effect, and creditors must stop all collection activity. The petition is the single most consequential document in a bankruptcy case -- everything flows from it.

What You Need to File

A complete voluntary petition package includes several documents. Not all are required at the moment of filing (see "Bare Petition Filing" below), but a complete filing includes:

The Petition -- Official Form 101 (individuals) or Official Form 201 (non-individuals). This is the core document identifying the debtor, the chapter, and basic case information.

Schedules A through J -- a comprehensive disclosure of assets, liabilities, income, and expenses. Schedule A/B covers property, Schedule C covers exemptions, Schedule D covers secured debts, Schedule E/F covers unsecured debts, and Schedules I and J cover current income and expenses.

Statement of Financial Affairs (SOFA) -- Official Form 107. A detailed questionnaire covering income history, property transfers, lawsuits, financial accounts, and other information relevant to the case.

Means Test (Chapter 7 only) -- Official Form 122A. Determines whether the debtor qualifies for Chapter 7 or must file under Chapter 13 based on income and expenses.

Credit Counseling Certificate -- proof that the debtor completed an approved credit counseling course within 180 days before filing (see below).

Additional documents may be required by local rules depending on the court. Many courts require a creditor matrix (mailing list), a statement of social security number, and a statement of current monthly income.

Filing Fees

Each chapter has a different filing fee set by the Judicial Conference:

Chapter 7: $338

Chapter 13: $313

Chapter 11: $1,738

Fee waivers are available for Chapter 7 individual debtors whose household income is below 150% of the federal poverty guidelines and who cannot pay the fee in installments. The debtor must file an application (Official Form 103B) and the court decides.

Installment payments are available in all chapters for individuals. The debtor files an application (Official Form 103A) proposing to pay the filing fee in up to four installments within 120 days of filing. The court may extend this to 180 days for cause.

Credit Counseling Requirement

Section 109(h) requires individual debtors to complete a credit counseling briefing from an approved agency within 180 days before filing the petition. This is a strict eligibility requirement -- a case filed without the certificate (or an exemption) can be dismissed.

The briefing typically takes 60 to 90 minutes and can be completed online, by phone, or in person. The cost is usually $10 to $50, and agencies must provide free services to debtors who cannot pay.

A list of approved credit counseling agencies is available at justice.gov. The certificate must be filed with the petition or shortly after.

Exemptions exist for active military personnel deployed in combat zones, debtors who are incapacitated or disabled, and situations where the debtor requested counseling but could not obtain it within 7 days of the request.

What Happens When You File

Filing the voluntary petition triggers several immediate consequences:

The automatic stay takes effect. Under Section 362, nearly all creditor collection activity must stop the instant the petition is filed -- lawsuits, garnishments, foreclosures, repossessions, phone calls, and letters. Learn more at automaticstay.org.

A case number is assigned. The clerk assigns a case number and the case is assigned to a bankruptcy judge. All future filings reference this case number.

The 341 meeting is scheduled. The court schedules a meeting of creditors (also called the 341 meeting) typically 20 to 40 days after filing. The debtor must attend and answer questions under oath from the trustee and any creditors who appear. Learn more at 341meeting.org.

Creditors are notified. The Bankruptcy Noticing Center (BNC) mails notice to all creditors listed in the petition, informing them of the case, the automatic stay, and key deadlines.

A trustee is appointed. In Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 cases, a trustee is assigned to administer the case. In Chapter 11, the debtor typically remains in possession unless cause exists for a trustee.

Bare Petition Filing

In an emergency, a debtor can file just the petition itself -- without schedules, the SOFA, or other documents. This is called a "bare petition" or "skeleton filing." It is authorized by Bankruptcy Rule 1007(c).

A bare petition filing triggers the automatic stay immediately, which is why it is used in emergencies -- for example, to stop a foreclosure sale, a wage garnishment, or a vehicle repossession that is about to happen.

The 14-day deadline. After a bare petition filing, the debtor must file all remaining required documents within 14 days. If the debtor fails to complete the filing within that window, the court may dismiss the case without further notice. Some courts grant extensions, but this is not guaranteed.

Bare petition filings carry risk. An incomplete filing can result in dismissal, which lifts the automatic stay and may create complications for future filings under Section 109(g). They should be used only when the debtor truly needs emergency protection and intends to complete the filing promptly.
